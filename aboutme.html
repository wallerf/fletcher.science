Fletcher Waller

Masters Student

Fletcher Waller is a queer astrophysicist at the University of Victoria, where he studies nearby ancient galaxies and their stars. Astronomy has been a lifelong interest for Fletcher. The best gift he ever recieved was at 8 years old from his older brother: a build-it-yourself size- and distance-scaled model solar system which hung on his bedroom ceiling for years.

Fletcher took an unusual path to being an astronomer, though. He started university as a music student, but whenever he wasn't playing or practicing his tuba, he was watching the Bad Astronomer's (Phil Plait's) Crash Course Astronomy on YouTube. Fletcher even named his tuba after the most massive star in the nearby galaxy: η Carinae. After ultimately deciding he preferred music as a passion and not a career, he started taking astronomy classes. Fletcher capped off his transition from music to astronomy when he played "The Planets" by Gustav Holst with the Victoria Symphony alongside his former tuba coach Paul Beauschesne, and with his at-the-time instructor for Planetary Science, Dr. Sam Lawler, in the audience. Shortly after, Sam offered Fletcher his first astronomy research position, in which he ran n-body simulations to test for the presence of planets around the star Fomalhaut.

Another pivotal moment in Fletcher's astronomy undergrad career was in 2017, when the first neutron star-neutron star merger, GW170817, was announced by LIGO. He was fascinated by the medium of gravitational wave astronomy, and was even more enamoured by how NS-NS mergers were perfect targets for multi-messenger astronomy. He went to another one of his professors, Dr. Kim Venn, to talk about it, and she was more than happy to explain how paradigm-changing this discovery was in terms of understanding where some of the heaviest metals, like gold, come from.

Fast forward a couple of years, and Kim Venn offered Fletcher his second astronomy research position, in which he measured heavy metal compositions of stars in the Milky Way's galactic bulge. He went on to start his current masters degree supervised by Dr. Venn. His research work focuses on high-resolution spectroscopic observations of stars in nearby Ultra-Faint Dwarf galaxies, and using the spectral fingerprints of atoms to determine the compositions of those stars. These galaxies are ancient; they stopped forming new stars 1-2 billion years after the Big Bang. And they are also very nearby: they are closer to us than the Andromeda galaxy. This makes Ultra-Faint Dwarfs very valuable for the study of the very early universe, and help us decode what the first stars and supernovae looked like.

During his masters, Fletcher is also a NTCO student (New Technologies for Canadian Observatories, an organization that bridges the gap between academia and industry at early astronomy career stages). His research focus on high-resolution spectroscopy helped him get an internship at the Gemini South Telescope in Chile, working on the brand new GHOST spectrograph. Fletcher is testing the data reduction pipeline, selecting targets for some of GHOST's first observations, and doing everything he can to make sure GHOST is a great instrument for the broader astronomical community.
